<h2>Discussion</h2>
<p>Most of the analysts rely on Yelp for their predictions (14 out of 24 specifically mention Yelp). An article at fivethirtyeight.com claims that &ldquo;there&rsquo;s a reasonably strong correlation between Yelp and Michelin ratings.&rdquo; <a href="http://fivethirtyeight.com/features/yelp-and-michelin-have-the-same-taste-in-new-york-restaurants/">http://fivethirtyeight.com/features/yelp-and-michelin-have-the-same-taste-in-new-york-restaurants/</a></p>
<h2>Yelpers</h2>
<p>Several competitors augment Yelp with other data sources.</p>
<ul>
<li>Brendan Sudol (the contest winner) searches Zagat, the Washingtonian, and reviews by Tom Sietsema of the Washington Post.</li>
<li>Jon Mclaughlin (upper 25%) uses Yelp and Zagat.</li>
<li>Bertrand Dolimier (who ranks in the upper half) uses Yelp, Zagat, Google and various newspaper reviews from the U.S. Michelin cities (New York, Chicago, San Francisco, DC).</li>
<li>Cheng Ji (upper half) scrapes top restaurant lists from Chicago, New York, San Francisco, also the Washingtonian and the Washington Post, before turning to Yelp.</li>
<li>Eugene Kim uses Yelp supplemented with DC restaurant health inspector data on OpenDataDC.(lowest rank).</li>
</ul>
<h2>Non-Yelpers</h2>
<p>But a few analysts eschew Yelp:</p>
<ul>
<li>Erin Lynch-Klarup relies on Zagat data (and gains third place!)</li>
<li>Local Micro uses the Washingtonian and the Washington Post along with a couple of blogs (and ranks at the bottom of the upper 25%).</li>
<li>Michael Gasparovic (upper half) prefers the Washington Post, the Washingtonian, and Todd Kliman.</li>
<li>Sam Stack uses the Washingtonian 100 Best for 2016 (upper two-thirds)</li>
<li>Mickin Sani (upper two-thirds) refers to the Washington Post and winners of the James Beard Award, but doesn&rsquo;t mention other sources.</li>
<li>An Nguyen (ranking in the lower half) appears to be using Gayot and Zagat.</li>
<li>Kulwant Saluja (bottom 25%) starts with Yelp, decides not to use it, tries Zagat and then uses a list of New York City top 50 restaurants, and the Washington Post reviews for DC.</li>
<li>Sheena Lee Villanueva (bottom 25%) uses Google scraping and Zagat, but does not reference any other source of data.</li>
<li>Stephen Keller (bottom 25%) just says &ldquo;a recent top 100 list.&rdquo;</li>
</ul>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p>In the ranking used in this analysis, two of the top six contestants did not use Yelp.&nbsp; Erin Lynch-Klarup used Zagat against Michelin data for other U.S. cities; Local Micro used the Washingtonian restaurant review database.</p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p>An important factor for success in this competition is correctly choosing the &lsquo;size of the box&rsquo; &mdash; the quantity of restaurants that will receive Michelin stars. The readmes and whitepapers don&rsquo;t provide enough information to determine if the contestants were limiting the size of prediction lists based on existing data from other Michelin guide cities (New York, Chicago, San Francisco) or if they used the DC Bib Gourmand list to exclude restaurants.</p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p>In any case, using Yelp to predict Michelin stars is helpful, but it is not necessary for a high rank in the contest.</p>
